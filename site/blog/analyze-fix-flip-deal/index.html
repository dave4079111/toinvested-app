How to Analyze a Fix & Flip Deal Like a Pro

By David J. Moore February 24, 2026 10 min read

Fix and flip investing can be incredibly profitable — or devastatingly expensive. The difference comes down to analysis. A great flipper doesn't just have good design taste; they have airtight numbers before they ever swing a hammer.

The 70% Rule: Your First Filter

The 70% rule is the quickest way to screen a flip deal:

Maximum Purchase Price = (ARV × 70%) - Repair Costs

ARV (After Repair Value) is what the property will be worth after renovations, based on comparable sales. The 30% margin covers your holding costs, selling costs, and profit.

70% Rule Example

ARV: $350,000

Estimated repairs: $60,000

Max purchase price: ($350,000 × 0.70) - $60,000 = $185,000

If you can buy for $185K or less, the deal has solid profit potential.

Step 1: Determine the After Repair Value (ARV)

ARV is the foundation of every flip analysis. Get this wrong, and nothing else matters.

  • Pull comparable sales — find 3-5 similar renovated homes that sold within 6 months and 0.5 miles
  • Match condition level — your comps should reflect the same renovation quality you plan
  • Adjust for differences — add/subtract for bedrooms, bathrooms, square footage, lot size
  • Be conservative — use the median, not the highest comp. Markets can shift during your renovation

Step 2: Estimate Repair Costs

Walk the property with a contractor (or develop your own cost knowledge) and estimate every item:

Renovation ItemTypical Cost Range
Kitchen remodel (mid-range)$15,000 - $35,000
Bathroom remodel$5,000 - $15,000 each
Flooring (whole house)$5,000 - $12,000
Interior paint$3,000 - $6,000
Roof replacement$8,000 - $15,000
HVAC replacement$5,000 - $10,000
Electrical update$3,000 - $8,000
Plumbing repair$2,000 - $8,000
Foundation repair$5,000 - $25,000+
Landscaping$2,000 - $5,000

Pro tip: Add a 10-15% contingency buffer. Every flip has surprises — hidden water damage, outdated wiring, or structural issues you couldn't see during the initial walkthrough.

Step 3: Calculate Holding Costs

Holding costs are the silent killer of flip profits. Every month you hold the property, these expenses pile up:

  • Mortgage/hard money interest — often $1,000-$2,500/month on a hard money loan
  • Property taxes — prorated monthly
  • Insurance — builder's risk or vacant property policy
  • Utilities — electric, water, gas during renovation
  • HOA fees — if applicable

Holding Cost Example (6-Month Hold)

Hard money interest: $1,800/mo × 6 = $10,800

Property taxes: $350/mo × 6 = $2,100

Insurance: $150/mo × 6 = $900

Utilities: $200/mo × 6 = $1,200

Total holding costs: $15,000

Step 4: Calculate Selling Costs

  • Agent commissions: 5-6% of sale price
  • Closing costs: 1-2% (title, transfer taxes, etc.)
  • Staging: $2,000-$5,000 (optional but recommended)
  • Seller concessions: 0-3% (buyer may request credits)

On a $350,000 sale, expect $21,000-$28,000 in selling costs.

Step 5: Calculate Your Profit

Complete Flip Analysis

ARV (sale price): $350,000

Purchase price: -$185,000

Renovation costs: -$60,000

Holding costs (6 months): -$15,000

Selling costs (7%): -$24,500

Acquisition costs (2%): -$3,700

Net profit: $61,800

ROI: $61,800 ÷ $248,700 (total invested) = 24.8% in 6 months

Red Flags: When to Walk Away

  1. Foundation issues — unpredictable costs that can blow your budget
  2. Environmental hazards — asbestos, lead paint, mold remediation is expensive
  3. Zoning or permitting problems — verify before buying
  4. Thin margins — if profit is under $30,000, one surprise can wipe you out
  5. Declining market — your ARV may be lower by the time you sell

Exit Strategies Beyond Selling

Smart flippers always have a backup plan:

  • Rent it out — if the market softens, hold as a rental until conditions improve
  • BRRRR it — refinance based on the new ARV and keep as a long-term rental (learn more)
  • Seller financing — sell with owner financing for ongoing income and higher total return

Key Takeaways

  • Use the 70% rule for quick deal screening: Max Price = (ARV × 70%) - Repairs
  • Always get 3-5 comparable sales to verify ARV — conservative is better
  • Budget 10-15% contingency above your renovation estimate
  • Holding costs of $2,000-$4,000/month can destroy profits on slow projects
  • Target minimum $30,000+ profit per flip to account for unexpected costs
