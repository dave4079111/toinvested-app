Cap Rate Explained: What Every Investor Must Know

By David J. Moore February 15, 2026 8 min read

The capitalization rate (cap rate) is one of the most commonly used metrics in real estate investing — and one of the most misunderstood. Knowing how to use cap rates properly will help you compare deals, evaluate markets, and avoid overpaying for properties.

What Is Cap Rate?

Cap rate measures the unlevered return on a property — the return you'd earn if you paid all cash with no mortgage.

Cap Rate = Net Operating Income (NOI) ÷ Purchase Price × 100

Net Operating Income (NOI) = Gross rental income minus all operating expenses (excluding mortgage payments). Operating expenses include property taxes, insurance, maintenance, property management, vacancy allowance, and utilities paid by the owner.

Cap Rate Calculation Example

Calculating Cap Rate

Purchase price: $250,000

Annual gross rent: $30,000

Vacancy (5%): -$1,500

Operating expenses: -$10,500 (taxes, insurance, maintenance, management)

NOI: $18,000

Cap Rate: $18,000 ÷ $250,000 = 7.2%

What Is a Good Cap Rate?

Cap rates vary dramatically by market, property type, and condition:

Market TypeTypical Cap RateExamples
Class A (Prime urban)3-5%Manhattan, San Francisco, Miami Beach
Class B (Suburban growth)5-7%Nashville, Charlotte, Phoenix suburbs
Class C (Working class)7-10%Midwest cities, secondary markets
Class D (Distressed)10%+High-vacancy areas, declining neighborhoods

Important: A higher cap rate doesn't automatically mean a better deal. High cap rates often correlate with higher risk — lower appreciation potential, more management-intensive tenants, and greater vacancy risk.

Cap Rate vs. Cash-on-Cash Return

These metrics answer different questions:

MetricCap RateCash-on-Cash Return
Financing?Ignores financingIncludes mortgage payments
MeasuresProperty's unlevered returnYour actual return on cash invested
Best forComparing properties, evaluating marketsEvaluating your personal ROI
FormulaNOI ÷ PriceCash Flow ÷ Cash Invested

Use cap rate to compare properties on an apples-to-apples basis. Use cash-on-cash return to evaluate how a specific deal performs with your financing.

When Cap Rate Matters Most

  • Comparing multiple properties — cap rate normalizes the comparison by removing financing variables
  • Evaluating markets — average cap rates tell you if a market is expensive (low cap) or affordable (high cap)
  • Commercial property valuation — commercial properties are literally priced based on cap rate (Price = NOI ÷ Cap Rate)
  • All-cash purchases — if you're paying cash, cap rate IS your return

When Cap Rate Doesn't Tell the Full Story

  • Value-add opportunities — a property with 5% cap rate might become 8% after renovations and rent increases
  • Appreciation markets — low cap rates in Austin or Boise are offset by 5-10% annual appreciation
  • Leveraged returns — financing magnifies returns beyond what cap rate shows
  • Tax benefits — depreciation and other tax advantages don't appear in cap rate calculations

Cap Rate Compression and Expansion

Cap Rate Compression

When cap rates decrease (prices go up relative to income), it means investors are willing to pay more per dollar of income — usually because of:

  • Low interest rates making financing cheap
  • High demand for properties in growing markets
  • Expectations of future rent growth and appreciation

Cap Rate Expansion

When cap rates increase (prices drop relative to income), it signals:

  • Rising interest rates making deals less attractive
  • Economic uncertainty or declining demand
  • Better buying opportunities for investors — you get more income per dollar

Key Takeaways

  • Cap rate = NOI ÷ Purchase Price, measuring the property's unlevered return
  • Good cap rates range from 5-10% depending on market class and risk profile
  • Higher cap rates mean higher returns but usually come with higher risk
  • Use cap rate to compare properties; use cash-on-cash return to evaluate your personal ROI
  • Cap rate expansion (rising cap rates) often creates the best buying opportunities
