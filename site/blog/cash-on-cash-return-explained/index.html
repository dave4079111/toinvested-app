Cash-on-Cash Return Explained: Calculate Real Estate ROI

By David J. Moore March 15, 2026 9 min read

Cash-on-cash return is the single most important metric for rental property investors. It tells you exactly how much income you earn relative to the actual cash you invested — not the total property value, not the bank's money, but your money.

Unlike cap rate, which ignores financing, cash-on-cash return reflects the reality of leveraged investing. In this guide, we'll break down the formula, walk through real examples, and show you how to use this metric to spot winning deals.

What Is Cash-on-Cash Return?

Cash-on-cash return (CoC) measures the annual pre-tax cash flow generated by a property relative to the total cash you invested out of pocket. It answers a simple question: "For every dollar I put in, how many cents am I earning back each year?"

Cash-on-Cash Return = Annual Pre-Tax Cash Flow ÷ Total Cash Invested × 100

This metric is especially powerful because it accounts for your financing terms. Two identical properties can produce wildly different CoC returns depending on your down payment, interest rate, and loan structure.

How to Calculate Cash-on-Cash Return

Step 1: Calculate Annual Pre-Tax Cash Flow

Start with your gross rental income and subtract all expenses:

  • Gross rental income — total annual rent collected
  • Vacancy allowance — typically 5-8% of gross rent
  • Operating expenses — property taxes, insurance, maintenance, property management, utilities, HOA
  • Debt service — annual mortgage payments (principal + interest)

The result is your Net Operating Income minus debt service = Annual Pre-Tax Cash Flow.

Step 2: Calculate Total Cash Invested

Add up every dollar that came out of your pocket:

  • Down payment
  • Closing costs — typically 2-5% of purchase price
  • Renovation costs — any upfront repairs or improvements
  • Reserves — initial cash set aside for the property

Step 3: Divide and Multiply

Real-World Example

Purchase price: $200,000 | Down payment (25%): $50,000 | Closing costs: $6,000 | Repairs: $4,000

Total Cash Invested: $60,000

Monthly rent: $1,800 | Annual gross rent: $21,600

Vacancy (5%): -$1,080 | Operating expenses: -$7,200 | Annual mortgage: -$9,600

Annual Pre-Tax Cash Flow: $3,720

Cash-on-Cash Return: $3,720 ÷ $60,000 = 6.2%

What Is a Good Cash-on-Cash Return?

The answer depends on your market and strategy, but here are general benchmarks:

CoC ReturnRatingContext
< 4%Below AverageMay not justify the effort and risk vs passive investments
4-6%AcceptableCommon in expensive coastal markets with strong appreciation
6-10%GoodSweet spot for most buy-and-hold investors
10-15%ExcellentAchievable in Midwest and Southern markets
15%+OutstandingOften involves value-add or creative financing strategies

Remember: a lower CoC return in an appreciating market (like Austin or Nashville) might outperform a higher CoC return in a stagnant market when you factor in equity growth.

Cash-on-Cash Return vs. Other Metrics

CoC Return vs. Cap Rate

Cap rate ignores financing entirely — it divides NOI by purchase price. This makes it useful for comparing properties on a level playing field, but it doesn't reflect YOUR actual return. CoC return factors in your specific loan terms, making it more personally relevant.

CoC Return vs. Total ROI

Total ROI includes appreciation, mortgage paydown, and tax benefits. CoC return only measures cash flow, making it more conservative but more immediately tangible. Many investors use CoC as their primary screening metric, then calculate total ROI for final decisions.

CoC Return vs. IRR

Internal rate of return (IRR) accounts for the time value of money across a projected holding period. CoC return is a snapshot of year-one performance. Both have their place — use CoC for quick deal screening and IRR for long-term hold analysis.

How to Improve Your Cash-on-Cash Return

There are several levers you can pull:

  • Negotiate a lower purchase price — every dollar saved increases your return
  • Increase rent — renovations, better marketing, or adding amenities
  • Reduce expenses — shop insurance, contest property taxes, self-manage
  • Use better financing — lower rates, higher leverage, or creative financing
  • Add income streams — laundry, parking, storage, pet rent
  • House hack — live in one unit to eliminate your housing expense (see our guide)

Common Cash-on-Cash Mistakes

  1. Forgetting closing costs — these can be $5,000-$15,000 and dramatically affect your return
  2. Ignoring vacancy — no property stays 100% occupied forever
  3. Underestimating maintenance — budget 5-10% of rent for repairs and CapEx
  4. Using pro-forma rents — base calculations on actual market rents, not wishful thinking
  5. Not accounting for property management — even if you self-manage, your time has value

Key Takeaways

  • Cash-on-cash return measures your actual return on invested cash, accounting for financing
  • A good CoC return is typically 6-10% for buy-and-hold, though market context matters
  • Always include closing costs, vacancy, and maintenance in your calculations
  • Use CoC return for quick screening, then layer in appreciation and tax benefits for final analysis
  • Leverage, value-add renovations, and creative financing can significantly boost your CoC return
