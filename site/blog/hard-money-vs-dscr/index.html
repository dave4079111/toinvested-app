Hard Money vs. DSCR Loans: Which Is Right for You?

By David J. Moore February 5, 2026 8 min read

Hard money and DSCR loans are the two most popular non-conventional financing options for real estate investors — but they serve completely different purposes. Choosing the wrong one can cost you thousands. Here's how to pick the right tool for each deal.

Hard Money Loans at a Glance

Hard money loans are short-term, asset-based loans designed for properties that need renovation. The property's value (or future value) is the primary collateral.

  • Best for: Fix-and-flips, BRRRR deals, bridge financing
  • Loan term: 6-18 months
  • Interest rates: 10-14%
  • Origination points: 2-4 points
  • LTV: 65-75% of ARV
  • Closing speed: 7-14 days

DSCR Loans at a Glance

DSCR loans are long-term, income-based loans for stabilized rental properties. The property's rental income must cover the debt service.

  • Best for: Buy-and-hold rentals, refinancing after rehab
  • Loan term: 30 years (fixed or ARM)
  • Interest rates: 7-9.5%
  • Origination: 0.5-2 points
  • LTV: 75-80%
  • Closing speed: 21-30 days

When to Use Hard Money

  • Fix-and-flip projects — you need renovation funding and plan to sell within 6-12 months
  • BRRRR strategy (buy phase) — acquire and rehab, then refinance into DSCR
  • Time-sensitive deals — you need to close in under 2 weeks
  • Distressed properties — properties that won't qualify for conventional or DSCR financing
  • Auction purchases — when you need proof of funds quickly

When to Use DSCR

  • Buy-and-hold rentals — long-term hold of stabilized, rent-ready properties
  • BRRRR refinance — transitioning from hard money to permanent long-term financing
  • Portfolio scaling — adding properties beyond conventional loan limits (10+ properties)
  • Self-employed investors — your tax returns don't show enough qualifying income
  • Short-term rental properties — some DSCR lenders accept projected Airbnb income

The BRRRR Bridge: Hard Money → DSCR

The most common strategy combines both loan types:

  1. Acquire with hard money — fast closing, rehab funding included
  2. Renovate the property — use hard money draw schedule
  3. Rent the property — place a tenant at market rent
  4. Refinance into DSCR — based on new appraised value and rental income
  5. Repeat — recovered capital funds the next hard money purchase

Hard Money to DSCR Transition Example

Hard money loan: $135,000 at 12% (purchase $120K + rehab $15K funded)

Monthly hard money cost: $1,350/month (interest only)

After rehab ARV: $200,000

DSCR refinance (75% LTV): $150,000 at 7.5% for 30 years

New monthly payment: $1,049 (P&I)

Monthly rent: $1,700

DSCR: 1.62 — easily qualifies

Cash recovered: $150,000 - $135,000 payoff - $3,500 closing = $11,500 cash out

Common Mistakes

  1. Using hard money as permanent financing — 12% interest destroys cash flow long-term
  2. Using DSCR for a property needing major rehab — DSCR requires rent-ready condition
  3. Not shopping rates — hard money and DSCR rates vary 2-3% between lenders
  4. Ignoring prepayment penalties — DSCR prepayment penalties can cost $5,000-$15,000 if you sell early

Key Takeaways

  • Hard money is for short-term, value-add deals (flips and BRRRR acquisitions)
  • DSCR is for long-term buy-and-hold rentals and permanent financing
  • The best investors use both: hard money to acquire and DSCR to hold
  • Always plan your exit from hard money before closing — have a refinance or sale timeline
  • Shop multiple lenders for both loan types to find the best rates and terms
