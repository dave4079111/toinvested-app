What Is a 1031 Exchange? Tax-Free Real Estate Investing

By David J. Moore March 12, 2026 10 min read

The 1031 exchange is one of the most powerful wealth-building tools in real estate. Named after Section 1031 of the Internal Revenue Code, it allows you to defer all capital gains taxes when you sell an investment property — as long as you reinvest the proceeds into a "like-kind" property.

Think of it as a legal tax loophole that lets you trade up to bigger, better properties without the IRS taking a 15-30% bite out of your profits. Used strategically, 1031 exchanges can help you build a massive real estate portfolio while paying zero capital gains taxes along the way.

How a 1031 Exchange Works

At its core, a 1031 exchange is simple: sell one investment property, buy another of equal or greater value, and defer the taxes. But the execution requires precision.

The Basic Process

  1. Sell your relinquished property — the property you currently own
  2. Engage a Qualified Intermediary (QI) — they hold your sale proceeds (you cannot touch the money)
  3. Identify replacement properties — within 45 days of closing
  4. Close on replacement property — within 180 days of selling

The Critical Timelines

45-Day Identification Period

Starting from the day you sell, you have exactly 45 calendar days to formally identify potential replacement properties in writing to your QI. You can identify up to:

  • Three Property Rule: Up to 3 properties of any value
  • 200% Rule: Any number of properties, as long as their combined value doesn't exceed 200% of the sold property's value
  • 95% Rule: Any number of properties if you close on at least 95% of the identified value

Most investors use the Three Property Rule for simplicity.

180-Day Exchange Period

You must close on the replacement property within 180 calendar days of selling the relinquished property. This deadline is absolute — there are no extensions, even if day 180 falls on a weekend or holiday.

1031 Exchange Requirements

  • Like-kind property — any real property held for investment or business use qualifies. A single-family rental can be exchanged for a commercial building, raw land, or even a share in a Delaware Statutory Trust (DST)
  • Equal or greater value — the replacement property must be of equal or greater value to defer 100% of taxes. Buy for less, and you pay taxes on the difference (called "boot")
  • Same taxpayer — the person/entity on the sale must be the same on the purchase
  • Investment or business use — personal residences and properties held primarily for resale (flips) do not qualify
  • Qualified Intermediary required — you cannot hold the funds yourself

1031 Exchange Example

Trading Up: Duplex to 8-Unit

Sell: Duplex purchased for $200,000, now worth $350,000

Capital gain: $150,000 (simplified)

Tax owed without 1031: $150,000 × 25% = $37,500

With 1031 exchange: Reinvest full $350,000 equity into an 8-unit apartment building

Tax deferred: $37,500 stays invested and working for you

That $37,500 in an 8% cash-on-cash property generates an extra $3,000/year in cash flow — forever.

Types of 1031 Exchanges

Delayed (Forward) Exchange

The most common type. You sell first, then buy within 180 days. About 95% of all 1031 exchanges are delayed exchanges.

Reverse Exchange

You buy the replacement property before selling the relinquished property. More complex and expensive, but useful in hot markets where you can't risk losing the replacement property.

Improvement (Build-to-Suit) Exchange

Buy a property and use exchange funds to renovate it within the 180-day window. The improvements must be completed before the exchange period ends.

Common 1031 Exchange Mistakes

  1. Missing the 45-day deadline — this is the #1 reason exchanges fail. Mark the date immediately
  2. Touching the proceeds — if you receive any sale funds, even briefly, the exchange is disqualified
  3. Not reinvesting all equity — if you pull out cash, you'll pay taxes on the amount withdrawn
  4. Exchanging into a personal residence — the replacement must be held for investment
  5. Using the wrong intermediary — choose a bonded, insured QI with a strong track record
  6. Ignoring debt replacement — if you had a $150K mortgage, the replacement should have at least $150K in debt

1031 Exchange and Depreciation

When you do a 1031 exchange, the depreciation basis carries over to the new property. This means you can continue depreciating the property — and with a higher-value property, you may qualify for a cost segregation study that accelerates your deductions even further.

Can You 1031 Exchange Forever?

Yes — and this is where the real magic happens. You can perform unlimited 1031 exchanges throughout your lifetime, deferring taxes every time. When you pass away, your heirs receive a stepped-up basis, meaning the deferred gains are eliminated entirely. This is the ultimate generational wealth strategy.

Key Takeaways

  • 1031 exchanges let you defer 100% of capital gains taxes when selling investment property
  • You have 45 days to identify and 180 days to close on replacement properties
  • A Qualified Intermediary must hold the funds — you can never touch the money
  • The replacement property must be of equal or greater value to defer all taxes
  • Chain unlimited 1031 exchanges together, then use stepped-up basis at death to eliminate gains
