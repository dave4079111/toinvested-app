Tax Strategy Guide

The IRS Actually Wants You to Own Real Estate

The tax code is a 75,000-page instruction manual for building wealth. Real estate investors get more deductions, deferrals, and loopholes than any other asset class.

Disclaimer: This is educational content, not tax advice. Consult a qualified CPA or tax attorney for your specific situation.

7 Tax Tools That Build Wealth

Master these strategies and you will keep significantly more of every dollar you earn from real estate.

1

Depreciation

The IRS lets you deduct the "wear and tear" on your property over 27.5 years for residential and 39 years for commercial — even while the property appreciates in value. This phantom expense reduces your taxable income without costing you a dime in cash.

Live Math Example

Property Purchase Price$300,000
Land Value (excluded, ~20%)-$60,000
Depreciable Basis$240,000
Annual Depreciation (27.5 yrs)$8,727 / year
Tax Savings (24% bracket)$2,095 / year
2

1031 Exchange

Sell an investment property and defer ALL capital gains taxes by reinvesting into a "like-kind" property. You have 45 days to identify replacement properties and 180 days to close. Stack 1031 exchanges throughout your career to build a massive portfolio tax-free.

Live Math Example

Original Purchase Price$200,000
Sale Price (5 years later)$350,000
Capital Gain$150,000
Federal + State Tax (28% combined)$42,000
Tax Deferred via 1031$42,000 saved
3

Cost Segregation

An engineering study that reclassifies building components (carpeting, appliances, landscaping, parking lots) from 27.5-year to 5, 7, or 15-year property. Combined with bonus depreciation, you can take massive year-one deductions. Game-changing for high-income earners.

Live Math Example

Property Value (improvements)$800,000
Standard Year-1 Depreciation$29,091
Cost Seg Reclassified (35% of basis)$280,000
Year-1 Bonus Depreciation (60%)$168,000
Additional Year-1 Tax Savings (37%)$51,356
4

Capital Gains Optimization

Short-term gains (under 1 year) are taxed as ordinary income (up to 37%). Long-term gains (over 1 year) get preferential rates of 0%, 15%, or 20%. Timing your sales strategically can save you tens of thousands in taxes.

Live Math Example

Profit on Property Sale$100,000
Tax if Sold in 11 Months (37% ordinary)$37,000
Tax if Sold in 13 Months (15% LTCG)$15,000
Savings from Waiting 2 Extra Months$22,000
5

QBI Deduction (Section 199A)

Qualified Business Income deduction allows pass-through entity owners to deduct up to 20% of their qualified real estate income. If your rental activity qualifies as a trade or business, you can deduct 20% of that net rental income before calculating taxes.

Live Math Example

Net Rental Income (3 properties)$80,000
QBI Deduction (20%)$16,000
Tax Savings (24% bracket)$3,840 / year
6

Real Estate Professional Status (REPS)

If you spend 750+ hours per year AND more than half your working time in real estate activities, you can classify as a Real Estate Professional. This unlocks the ability to deduct rental losses against ALL income — including W-2, business, and investment income. Incredibly powerful for high earners with a spouse in real estate.

Live Math Example

W-2 Income (spouse)$250,000
Rental Losses (after depreciation)-$80,000
Without REPS: Losses suspended$0 offset
With REPS: Losses applied to W-2$80,000 offset
Tax Savings (35% bracket)$28,000 / year
7

Opportunity Zones

Invest capital gains into a Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF) within 180 days and defer those gains. Hold for 10+ years and pay ZERO capital gains on the Opportunity Zone investment's appreciation. One of the most powerful wealth-building tools in the tax code.

Live Math Example

Capital Gain from Sale$500,000
Invested in Opportunity Zone Fund$500,000
OZ Investment Value After 10 Years$1,200,000
Appreciation in OZ$700,000
Tax on $700K Appreciation (held 10+ yrs)$0

6 Tax Mistakes That Cost Investors Thousands

1. Not Tracking Mileage

Driving to properties, showings, hardware stores, and meetings is deductible at $0.67/mile. Most investors lose $3,000-$8,000/year in unclaimed mileage.

Fix: Use MileIQ or a dedicated mileage log from day one.

2. Missing the 1031 Deadline

You have exactly 45 days to identify and 180 days to close on replacement properties. Miss it by one day and you owe the full capital gains tax.

Fix: Start identifying replacement properties BEFORE you sell.

3. Skipping Cost Segregation

Every property over $500K in value likely justifies a cost segregation study. The study costs $5K-$15K but can generate $50K-$200K+ in first-year deductions.

Fix: Get a cost seg study on every property over $500K.

4. Commingling Personal & Business

Mixing personal and rental expenses in one bank account is an audit red flag and makes it nearly impossible to maximize deductions.

Fix: Separate bank accounts and credit cards for each entity.

5. Not Documenting REPS Hours

The IRS requires contemporaneous time logs to prove Real Estate Professional Status. Claiming REPS without documentation is an audit magnet.

Fix: Keep a daily time log. Use apps or spreadsheets consistently.

6. Using a Non-Specialist CPA

Your cousin's CPA who does W-2 returns will miss real estate-specific deductions. A generalist CPA can cost you $10K-$50K+/year in missed savings.

Fix: Hire a CPA who specializes in real estate investors.

⚠ Critical Warning: Never Hold Long-Term Rentals in an S-Corp

Holding rental properties inside an S-Corp creates a tax nightmare. You lose the ability to do 1031 exchanges, cannot take advantage of the step-up in basis at death, face double taxation on appreciated property distributions, and lose favorable capital gains rates. S-Corps are great for active businesses and flipping operations, but long-term buy-and-hold properties belong in LLCs taxed as partnerships or disregarded entities. This is one of the most expensive mistakes investors make — and it's nearly impossible to unwind cleanly.
